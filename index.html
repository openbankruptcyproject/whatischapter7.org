What Is Chapter 7 Bankruptcy?

Chapter 7 bankruptcy -- sometimes called "liquidation bankruptcy" or "straight bankruptcy" -- is a federal court proceeding governed by 11 U.S.C. Sections 701 through 784. It is the most common form of bankruptcy filed in the United States, accounting for roughly 60-70% of all consumer bankruptcy petitions each year.

In a Chapter 7 case, a court-appointed trustee reviews the debtor's assets to determine whether any nonexempt property exists that can be sold to pay creditors. If nonexempt assets exist, the trustee liquidates them and distributes the proceeds. In practice, however, the vast majority of consumer Chapter 7 cases -- approximately 95% -- are "no-asset" cases. The debtor keeps everything they own because all of their property falls within applicable exemptions.

In exchange for submitting to this process, the debtor receives a discharge -- a permanent federal court order that eliminates personal liability on most pre-petition debts. The discharge injunction under 11 U.S.C. Section 524 makes any post-discharge attempt to collect a discharged debt a violation of federal law.

Statutory basis: Chapter 7 of Title 11 of the United States Code, 11 U.S.C. Sections 701-784. The discharge provision is at 11 U.S.C. Section 727. Exceptions to discharge are at 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a).

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13

The key difference: Chapter 7 eliminates debts outright with no repayment plan, while Chapter 13 repayment plan guide requires 3 to 5 years of payments before any debts are discharged. Chapter 7 is faster (3-4 months vs. 3-5 years), but Chapter 13 lets you catch up on mortgage arrears and car payments while keeping property that might be at risk in Chapter 7. For a detailed comparison, see Chapter 7 vs Chapter 13 comparison.

Who Qualifies for Chapter 7?

Not everyone can file Chapter 7. Congress added the means test in 2005 through the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act (BAPCPA) to prevent higher-income filers from using Chapter 7 when they could afford a Chapter 13 repayment plan.

The Means Test -- Two Parts

The bankruptcy means test guide (11 U.S.C. Section 707(b)(2)) works in two stages:

Part 1: Income comparison. If your current monthly income (averaged over the 6 months before filing) is at or below the median income for a household of your size in your state, you pass automatically. You do not need to complete the second part. The median income figures are updated periodically by the U.S. Census Bureau and published by the U.S. Trustee Program.

Part 2: Disposable income calculation. If your income is above the state median, you must complete a detailed calculation of your monthly disposable income. This subtracts IRS-approved living expense allowances, actual secured debt payments, priority debts, and certain other expenses from your income. If the result shows you have little or no disposable income to fund a Chapter 13 plan, you still qualify for Chapter 7.

Important: The means test applies only to consumer debtors whose debts are primarily consumer debts (not business debts). If most of your debt is business-related, the means test does not apply and you can file Chapter 7 regardless of income.

Other Eligibility Requirements

  • Credit counseling: You must complete a credit counseling course from an approved provider within 180 days before filing (11 U.S.C. Section 109(h)).
  • Prior filing restrictions: Under Section 727(a)(8), you cannot receive a Chapter 7 discharge if you received a Chapter 7 discharge in a case filed within the previous 8 years. A prior Chapter 13 discharge bars a new Chapter 7 discharge for 6 years under Section 727(a)(9), with limited exceptions.
  • No recent dismissal for bad faith: If a prior bankruptcy case was dismissed within 180 days for certain reasons (willful failure to obey court orders or filing in bad faith), you may face restrictions under Section 109(g).

What Debts Does Chapter 7 Discharge?

Chapter 7 discharges most unsecured debts, meaning you are no longer personally liable for them after discharge. The creditor cannot sue you, garnish your wages, or otherwise attempt collection.

Typically Discharged

  • Credit card balances
  • Medical bills
  • Personal loans and payday loans
  • Past-due utility bills
  • Collection accounts
  • Deficiency balances after repossession
  • Judgments from lawsuits (most)
  • Lease break fees
  • Some older tax debts (3+ years)

NOT Discharged

  • Student loans (absent undue hardship)
  • Child support and alimony
  • Recent income taxes (less than 3 years)
  • Criminal fines and restitution
  • Debts from fraud or willful injury
  • DUI/DWI injury judgments
  • Government fines and penalties
  • Debts not listed on schedules
  • Certain condo/HOA fees

The full list of nondischargeable debts is at 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a). For a deeper look at debts that survive bankruptcy, see debts that cannot be discharged.

What About Secured Debts?

Chapter 7 discharges the personal liability on secured debts (car loans, mortgages), but the creditor's lien on the property survives. This means:

  • Surrender: You can give back the property and owe nothing more -- the deficiency is discharged.
  • Reaffirmation: You can sign a reaffirmation agreement to keep the property and continue making payments, but you remain personally liable.
  • Redemption: Under 11 U.S.C. Section 722, you can pay the creditor the current value of the collateral in a lump sum and keep the property free of the lien.

The 341 Meeting of Creditors

Every Chapter 7 case includes a 341 meeting of creditors, required by 11 U.S.C. Section 341(a). This is the one hearing where you must appear (the judge does not attend). Here is what happens:

1
Scheduling: The meeting is held 20 to 40 days after your petition is filed. You will receive a notice with the date, time, and location (many courts now allow telephone or video attendance).
2
Identity verification: Bring a government-issued photo ID and proof of your Social Security number. The trustee verifies your identity under oath before the meeting begins.
3
Trustee questions: The Chapter 7 trustee asks questions about your petition, schedules, and financial affairs. Typical questions cover whether you reviewed your filing, whether everything is accurate, whether you have any assets not listed, and whether you have transferred property recently.
4
Creditor questions: Creditors have the right to appear and ask questions, but they rarely do in consumer cases. Most 341 meetings last 5 to 10 minutes.
5
After the meeting: Creditors have 60 days from the 341 meeting date to file objections to discharge or to the dischargeability of specific debts. If no objections are filed, the court enters your discharge.

Practical tip: The 341 meeting is usually the only time you need to appear in court during a Chapter 7 case. It is not adversarial -- the trustee is not trying to catch you in a lie. Answer honestly, bring your documents, and the meeting will typically last under 10 minutes.

Chapter 7 Timeline -- How Long Does It Take?

Most Chapter 7 cases move from filing to discharge in approximately 3 to 4 months. Here is the typical timeline:

Event When
Petition filed Day 0 -- automatic stay protection in bankruptcy takes effect immediately
341 Meeting of Creditors 20-40 days after filing
Deadline for objections to discharge 60 days after 341 meeting
Financial management course due Before discharge (usually within 60 days of 341)
Discharge entered ~60 days after 341 meeting (if no objections)
Case closed Shortly after discharge (no-asset case) or after distribution (asset case)

Compare this to Chapter 13 repayment plan guide, which requires 36 to 60 months of plan payments before any discharge is entered. For most people, Chapter 7 is the fastest path to a financial fresh start.

Note: Asset cases take longer. If the trustee identifies nonexempt property to sell, the case stays open until the trustee completes the liquidation and distribution. This can add several months or longer.

7 Common Myths About Chapter 7 Bankruptcy

Myth

"You lose everything you own in Chapter 7."

Fact

About 95% of Chapter 7 cases are no-asset cases. Every state provides exemptions that protect essential property -- your home (up to a limit), a vehicle, clothing, household goods, retirement accounts, and more. The trustee can only sell property that exceeds exemption limits, and in the vast majority of cases, nothing exceeds those limits.

Myth

"Only irresponsible people file bankruptcy."

Fact

Research consistently shows the leading causes of bankruptcy are medical debt, job loss, and divorce -- not reckless spending. A 2019 study published in the American Journal of Public Health found that 66.5% of all bankruptcies were tied to medical issues. Bankruptcy is a legal tool built into the U.S. Constitution (Article I, Section 8, Clause 4) specifically to give honest debtors a fresh start.

Myth

"You can never get credit again after Chapter 7."

Fact

While Chapter 7 stays on your credit report for 10 years, its impact diminishes over time. Many filers receive credit card offers within months of discharge. Secured credit cards, credit-builder loans, and responsible credit use can begin rebuilding your score immediately. Most filers see meaningful credit improvement within 1 to 2 years.

Myth

"Filing bankruptcy means you failed."

Fact

Bankruptcy exists because Congress recognized that unmanageable debt prevents economic productivity. The discharge gives debtors a second chance to contribute to the economy rather than spending years or decades in a debt spiral. Notable companies that used bankruptcy to restructure include General Motors, Marvel, and Delta Air Lines.

Myth

"Everyone will know you filed bankruptcy."

Fact

Bankruptcy filings are public records on PACER, but they do not appear in newspapers, are not broadcast publicly, and most people in your life will never know unless you tell them. Employers cannot fire you solely because you filed bankruptcy (11 U.S.C. Section 525), and government agencies cannot deny you a license or permit for it.

Myth

"You need a lawyer to file Chapter 7."

Fact

You have the right to file bankruptcy pro se (without an attorney). The court forms are available for free on uscourts.gov. However, bankruptcy law is complex, and mistakes can result in losing property or having your case dismissed. Many legal aid organizations provide free or low-cost help. Whether you need a lawyer depends on the complexity of your situation.

Myth

"Married couples must file together."

Fact

Married couples can file jointly or individually. Filing individually may make sense if only one spouse has significant debt, if you want to protect a spouse's credit, or if you have complex property situations. However, both spouses' income is considered on the means test even when only one files, because it uses "household income."

The Automatic Stay -- Immediate Protection

One of the most powerful features of Chapter 7 is the automatic stay protection in bankruptcy under 11 U.S.C. Section 362. The moment your petition is filed, the stay goes into effect and immediately prohibits:

  • Wage garnishment
  • Bank account levies and freezes
  • Creditor lawsuits and collection activity
  • Phone calls, letters, and emails from collectors
  • Repossession of vehicles (if not yet repossessed)
  • Foreclosure proceedings (temporarily)
  • Utility disconnection (for 20 days, with deposit)

Creditors who violate the automatic stay can be held in contempt of court and may be liable for damages, attorney fees, and punitive sanctions. The stay remains in effect until the case is closed, dismissed, or discharge is entered -- whichever comes first.

Exception for repeat filers: If you had a prior bankruptcy case dismissed within the past year, the automatic stay may only last 30 days (11 U.S.C. Section 362(c)(3)) or may not go into effect at all (Section 362(c)(4)). This is designed to prevent abuse through serial filings.

What Happens to Your Property?

When you file Chapter 7, all your property becomes part of the bankruptcy "estate." The trustee's job is to identify any nonexempt assets, sell them, and distribute the proceeds to creditors. But exemptions protect most property for most filers.

Key Exemption Categories

  • Homestead: Protects equity in your primary residence. Amounts vary widely by state -- from a few thousand dollars to unlimited (Texas, Florida, Kansas).
  • Motor vehicle: Most states protect $2,500 to $7,500 in vehicle equity. Some states allow higher amounts for older or disabled filers.
  • Retirement accounts: 401(k)s, IRAs, pensions, and other qualified retirement accounts are generally fully exempt under ERISA and 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d)(12).
  • Personal property: Clothing, household furniture, appliances -- most states exempt these up to reasonable amounts.
  • Wildcard: Some states (and the federal exemptions) provide a "wildcard" exemption you can apply to any property.
  • Tools of the trade: Equipment, tools, and books needed for your profession or trade are often exempt.

For detailed exemption information, see Federal Exemptions, Homestead Exemption, Vehicle Exemptions, and Wildcard Exemption on whatischapter7.com.

Filing Restrictions and Timing Bars

Federal law imposes mandatory waiting periods between bankruptcy discharges. These are strictly enforced and cannot be waived by the court:

Prior Case New Case Wait Period Statute
Chapter 7 Chapter 7 8 years 727(a)(8)
Chapter 13 Chapter 7 6 years 727(a)(9)
Chapter 7 Chapter 13 4 years 1328(f)(1)
Chapter 13 Chapter 13 2 years 1328(f)(2)

These waiting periods run from filing date to filing date (for Chapter 7-to-7) or filing date to filing date (for the 1328(f) bars). Getting the dates wrong can result in a case being filed, fees being paid, and then the discharge being denied months later. How Much Does Chapter 7 Cost?

The costs of filing Chapter 7 break down into three categories:

Court Filing Fee

The current filing fee for a Chapter 7 case is $338 (as of 2024). You can request to pay in installments (up to 4 payments over 120 days), and in cases of extreme hardship, the court may waive the fee entirely under 28 U.S.C. Section 1930(f).

Credit Counseling and Debtor Education

You must complete two courses: a pre-filing credit counseling course and a post-filing financial management (debtor education) course. Each typically costs $10 to $50, and fee waivers are available for low-income filers. Both courses can be completed online in about 60 to 90 minutes each.

Attorney Fees

If you hire an attorney, Chapter 7 fees typically range from $1,000 to $2,500 depending on your location and case complexity. Attorney fees in Chapter 7 must generally be paid before filing because post-petition debts to your attorney could be discharged. Some legal aid organizations provide free representation, and you always have the right to file pro se.
